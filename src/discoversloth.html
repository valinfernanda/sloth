Sloths are unique, slow-moving mammals native to the tropical rainforests of Central and South America. They are famous for their extremely deliberate movements and mostly arboreal (tree-dwelling) lifestyle.

Feature Details
Classification Mammal, belonging to the superorder Xenarthra (along with anteaters and armadillos).
Families/Types There are two families: Two-toed sloths (genus Choloepus) and Three-toed sloths (genus Bradypus). Note: All sloths have three toes on their hind limbs; the count refers to the fingers on their forelimbs.
Habitat Canopy of lowland tropical rainforests.
Movement The slowest mammal on Earth. They move only about 40 yards per day in the canopy. On the ground, they are virtually helpless, but they are surprisingly strong swimmers.
Diet Primarily folivores, meaning they eat leaves, buds, and twigs. Two-toed sloths have a more varied, omnivorous diet including some insects and fruit.
Metabolism & Digestion They have an exceptionally low metabolic rate and a multi-chambered stomach to digest tough cellulose. Digestion can take up to a month to complete for a single meal.
Sleep Wild sloths typically sleep around 8-10 hours per day, often curled up or hanging upside down.
Unique Anatomy Sloths spend nearly 90% of their lives hanging upside down. Their internal organs are attached to their ribcage, which prevents them from weighing down on their lungs. Three-toed sloths have 8 or 9 neck vertebrae (most mammals have 7), allowing them to rotate their heads up to 270 degrees.
Fur Their shaggy fur often hosts symbiotic algae, which gives them a greenish tint for camouflage, and is also home to various insects like the unique sloth moth.
Excretion They only descend to the forest floor to urinate and defecate about once a week, which is a vulnerable time for them. They can lose up to a third of their body weight in one sitting.

The health of the sloth population is directly tied to the health of the tropical rainforests.

  • Threats: The primary threat is deforestation and habitat fragmentation, which removes their food source and shelter. They are also vulnerable to predators (jaguars, harpy eagles) when they are on the forest floor.
  • Species Status: While some species, like the brown-throated sloth, are currently listed as 'Least Concern,' others like the Pygmy three-toed sloth are Critically Endangered.
